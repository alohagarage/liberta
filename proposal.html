Background and Significance

Introduction

Background on Futurism

Futurism's formal beginning was in 1909, heralded by the publication of the Futurist Manifesto in the French newspaper Le Figaro. The Manifesto proclaimed a firm opposition to all that was old, traditional and staid about Italian art and society. In no particular order, they promised to

  1. We want to sing the love of danger, the habit of energy and rashness.
  2. The essential elements of our poetry will be courage, audacity and revolt.
  3. Literature has up to now magnified pensive immobility, ecstasy and slumber. We want to exalt movements of aggression, feverish sleeplessness, the double march, the perilous leap, the slap and the blow with the fist.
  4. We declare that the splendor of the world has been enriched by a new beauty: the beauty of speed. A racing automobile with its bonnet adorned with great tubes like serpents with explosive breath ... a roaring motor car which seems to run on machine-gun fire, is more beautiful than the Victory of Samothrace.
  5. We want to sing the man at the wheel, the ideal axis of which crosses the earth, itself hurled along its orbit.
  6. The poet must spend himself with warmth, glamour and prodigality to increase the enthusiastic fervor of the primordial elements.
  7. Beauty exists only in struggle. There is no masterpiece that has not an aggressive character. Poetry must be a violent assault on the forces of the unknown, to force them to bow before man.
  8. We are on the extreme promontory of the centuries! What is the use of looking behind at the moment when we must open the mysterious shutters of the impossible? Time and Space died yesterday. We are already living in the absolute, since we have already created eternal, omnipresent speed.
  9. We want to glorify war — the only cure for the world — militarism, patriotism, the destructive gesture of the anarchists, the beautiful ideas which kill, and contempt for woman.
  10. We want to demolish museums and libraries, fight morality, feminism and all opportunist and utilitarian cowardice.
  11. We will sing of the great crowds agitated by work, pleasure and revolt; the multi-colored and polyphonic surf of revolutions in modern capitals: the nocturnal vibration of the arsenals and the workshops beneath their violent electric moons: the gluttonous railway stations devouring smoking serpents; factories suspended from the clouds by the thread of their smoke; bridges with the leap of gymnasts flung across the diabolic cutlery of sunny rivers: adventurous steamers sniffing the horizon; great-breasted locomotives, puffing on the rails like enormous steel horses with long tubes for bridle, and the gliding flight of aeroplanes whose propeller sounds like the flapping of a flag and the applause of enthusiastic crowds.

What was remarkable for the time, though, was the vehemence with which this proclamation was made. While many an artistic movement had made heady claims about the power of art and the desire for a utopian future, many of the movements had done so under the aegis of Romanticism or L'art pour l'art.

And while the conjunction of avant-garde art and pedagogy may seem as likely a meeting as, say, an umbrella and a sewing machine on an operating table, I think that a strong case can be made for the essentially educational aims of the Futurists. In Marinetti's writings, the Italian makes reference to what he calls a "Total Revolution" in Italy. By this, he means not only a superficial political revolution, but a revolution that would also remake the souls of Italian people and help them prepare for a different future. We can see that the aim of Futurism was never solely to entertain people, but to get them to think differently. And isn't this, ultimately, the goal of education as well?

Overview of Futurist Poetics

Although Futurism was a complex movement with several variants, we can isolate three main features of their poetics that are promising for the teaching of complex grammatical competencies.
  • Play

    While subsequent avant-garde movements, such as Dada from Switzerland and the more well-known French Surrealism are more commonly identified as "playful" —Most likely because of the Futurists' warlike tone— the Futurists also held play and the ludic in high esteem. One need only reference their famous Soiree Futuriste with their wild antics and biting satire.

    Perhaps more importantly than play as such, the Futurists were attracted to one of play's constituent elements: chance.

    Conflict

    While the importance of play, chance and risk in art may now seem obvious, what may not have seemed obvious at the time was the pedagogical value of chance. But there are have been several studies on importance of play and chance in pedagogy.

  • Technology

    Thematically, technology was extremely important for the Futurists. A cursory glance at their work, in both writing and painting, will note the prevalence of trains, automobiles, factories and airplanes. And while the idea of someone singing an ode to the automobile now seems either banal or quaint, at the beginning of the 20th century, it was unheard of. On the rare occasion that any artifact of modern technology made it into a poem, it served as a foil; it was used to disparage the modern world.

    But technology was not just a theme for the Futurists. I would argue that technology was actually the impetus for the movement as a whole. It was technology that set in motion the massive societal shifts that Futurism was responding to: mass migration, new forms of warfare, speed of industrial change. And it was this fervent new world that the Futurists hoped to prepare the Italian people for. What was radically different about the Futurists' stance on technology was their opinion that it was a force: neither good nor bad, it was a force that could be harnessed by the will of a person or a nation.

    The Futurists weren't only impelled by technology, they employed it to express themselves. In the field of literature, Marinetti and his cohort strained at the limits of typographic technology. The Futurist architect Alberto Sant'Elia imagined structures and possibilities for cities that are only now being realized. P. Russolo started the development of the synthesizer with his Intonarumori instruments and predicted the rise of new forms of atonal and electronic music in his treatise The Art of Noises.

    The Futurists used technology to prepare society for a more technological future. We find ourselves in a similar position with Liberta. What is shocking is that, in the period between the Futurists and ourselves, there has been very little in the way of investigation on the proper role and use of technology for learning. Technology, too often, is either explicitly or implicitly treated as a fad in learning. Prevalence here is a poor substitute for understanding. LIST OF STUDIES
